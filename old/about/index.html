Hi, I'm Paul

TL;DR: I'm an MS in computer science candidate @ UIUC. I have a deep passion for learning and building useful things.

In 2013, I graduated with a BSE in biomedical engineering from the University of Iowa. Early on during my undergrad, I spent my time volunteering and doing research. One summer I worked at the Mayo Clinic as a research assistant in Neuropathology, which was an incredibly rewarding experience. I was originally intending to pursue a medical degree, but I fell in love with building things after I started a company during my junior year.

Up until that point, the only programming experience I had was basic C and Matlab. Entering my senior year I decided to add a computer science minor and filled my schedule with CS and bioinformatics classes; basically, any class that involved programming and allowed me to graduate in time. I went to a couple hackathons that year where I learned so much more than I ever had in a single class. I applied for grad programs in CS and was luckily admitted into the program at UIUC.

At UIUC I'm advised by Prof. Sanmi Koyejo in the AI department. I spent my first semester as a CS 101 TA teaching Python and Matlab to freshman engineering students. I wanted to gain more hands-on experience so the next semester I worked as a research assistant for Prof. Kesh in the Health Care Engineering Systems Center. I was contributing to an open source project called Pulse. It's a physiology simulation engine and I contributed simulations of 3 valvular heart defects. My work was accepted as a poster at the 40th International Engineering in Medicine and Biology Conference. There's an exciting future in the physiology simulation space and a tremendous opportunity for human health impact.

That summer, I interned at Uptake, a Chicago based startup, as a software engineer. I worked on the competitive intelligence team building experimental features and I incorporated Node-RED and Orange into Uptake's platform allowing data science workflows to be built with no code. Industrial IoT is going to be a large space with customers realizing immediate returns.

For my Fall 2018 semester, I began consulting for OmniLife.ai, a startup out of Iowa City focused on improving clinical efficiency in transplant. I previously worked for them as an intern after I graduated from UIOWA and saw first-hand how technology could improve clinicians abilities ultimately improving patient outcomes. I primarily function as an ML researcher working on their SBIR Phase II research objectives. I'm building models that will ultimately reduce organ offer decision times and improve long-term graft survival. The ultimate hope is to improve outcomes across the transplant continuum improving patient quality of life.

There is a lot I want to accomplish in the future. For now, I'm awaiting the inevitable through experimentation.
